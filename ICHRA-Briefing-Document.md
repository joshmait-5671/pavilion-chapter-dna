## 1. What ICHRA Is

### Definition
An Individual Coverage Health Reimbursement Arrangement (ICHRA) is an employer-sponsored health benefit that allows businesses to provide employees with a defined, tax-free monthly allowance to purchase individual health insurance on the ACA Marketplace or off-exchange, rather than offering a traditional group health plan.

### Regulatory History

**Pre-ACA:** Employers commonly used HRAs to reimburse individual health insurance. The ACA (2010) inadvertently prohibited this, with employers facing excise taxes of $100/day/employee for reimbursing individual market premiums.

**2016 -- QSEHRA (Precursor):** The 21st Century Cures Act, signed by President Obama, created the Qualified Small Employer HRA. Limited to small employers with strict contribution caps, it proved too constrained for broad adoption.

**October 2017 -- Executive Order 13813:** President Trump issued "Promoting Healthcare Choice and Competition Across the United States," directing agencies to expand HRA usability. This was the executive catalyst, not legislation.

**October 2018:** The Departments of Treasury, HHS, and Labor proposed new HRA regulations.

**June 13, 2019:** Final rules issued, creating two new HRA types: the Individual Coverage HRA and the Excepted Benefit HRA. Effective January 1, 2020.

**January 1, 2020:** ICHRA launched. HHS projected 800,000 employers would offer ICHRAs covering 11+ million employees within 5-10 years.

**Bipartisan durability:** The Biden administration kept ICHRA intact. The CHOICE Arrangement Act passed the House in 2023 (would have codified ICHRA into statute) but stalled in the Senate. In 2025, provisions to rebrand ICHRAs as "CHOICE Arrangements" were included in congressional proposals but ultimately removed before enactment.

**Key distinction:** ICHRA exists through executive regulatory action, not legislation. This makes it technically reversible but politically durable given bipartisan support.

### How It Works
1. Employer defines a monthly allowance (e.g., $500/month) by employee class (age, geography, FT/PT, etc.)
2. Employee shops for individual health insurance on the ACA Marketplace or off-exchange
3. Employee purchases a plan and submits proof of coverage
4. Employer reimburses the employee tax-free up to the defined amount
5. If the ICHRA is deemed "affordable," employees lose eligibility for ACA premium tax credits

## 2. Market Size and Adoption

### Current Enrollment (2025-2026)
- **HRA Council estimate:** ~500,000 to 1 million covered lives (employees + dependents) on ICHRAs and QSEHRAs combined
- **HealthSherpa estimate:** 400,000-800,000 using ICHRAs to pay for coverage in 2025, 2.8x more than the prior year
- **EBRI estimate:** 350,000-700,000 workers and dependents covered

**Context:** This is tiny against 150+ million people in traditional employer-sponsored group coverage.

### Growth Trajectory
- ICHRA adoption is up ~1,000% since 2020 launch
- 124% year-over-year membership growth from 2024 to 2025
- 34% increase among large employers (50+ FTEs); 52% among smaller employers
- 83% of employers offering ICHRA/QSEHRA in 2025 had NOT previously offered any health coverage
- 92% employer retention rate year-over-year
- Less than 10% of employers who adopted ICHRA have exited the model
- Oliver Wyman projects 52% CAGR, reaching 2-5 million covered lives in the near term
- HealthSherpa projects ICHRA participation could triple again by 2027

### Why It's "Early Innings"
1. **Massive gap vs. projections:** HHS projected 11 million by 2025; actual is under 1 million
2. **Awareness deficit:** 49% of benefits managers surveyed had never heard of ICHRA
3. **Broker skepticism:** Many brokers remain non-believers or lack the knowledge to guide clients
4. **Technology fragmentation:** 100+ bentech players, few with complete solutions
5. **Individual market limitations:** Scarcity of PPO networks, inconsistent options across states
6. **Approaching the "chasm":** The market is between early adoption and mainstream acceptance -- the next 2-3 years are decisive
7. **Potential recession catalyst:** EBRI notes a recession could be the trigger that pushes employers toward defined-contribution models, as it would be the first downturn with ACA guaranteed-issue protections in place

### The 401(k) Analogy
ICHRA is frequently compared to the shift from defined-benefit pensions to 401(k) plans. Both represent a move from employer-defined benefits to employer-defined contributions with employee choice. EBRI explicitly frames ICHRAs as "the next chapter" in this transition.

- Dual-support model: AI recommendations + licensed human producers

**Nexben** (Minneapolis, founded 2015)
- End-to-end platform connecting carriers, brokers, employers, employees
- Unique pre-tax payroll withholding solution (moves beyond traditional reimbursement)
- Serves enterprise partners, TPAs, carriers, SaaS providers

**Other notable players:** Zorro (founded 2022), Gravie (founded 2013), BenefitBay, zizzl health, Thatch, Alegeus

### The Role of Carriers

Carriers occupy a conflicted position:
- **Individual market carriers** (Centene, Oscar, Ambetter) are bullish -- ICHRA brings employer-subsidized members into their existing individual market book
- **Group market carriers** face a strategic dilemma -- promoting ICHRA risks cannibalizing their own group business
- Some carriers are creating off-exchange individual plans with PPO networks resembling group products
- Centene predicts 45% of the employer group market is at risk of ICHRA disruption
- Total national spending on ICHRA was $84B in 2021, projected to reach $170B by 2030

### The Role of Brokers

Brokers are simultaneously a growth bottleneck and a critical distribution channel:
- **Traditional model:** Brokers earn commissions from group carriers at placement and renewal, with predictable recurring income
- **ICHRA disruption:** The shift to individual market inherently disintermediates brokers; some platforms assume the broker-of-record role
- **Commission compression:** Brokers may be forced to split commissions with ICHRA platforms or lose them entirely
- **Emerging models:** PEPM (per-employee-per-month) advisory fees, shared commissions, technology licensing arrangements
- **Oliver Wyman segmentation:** Progressive brokers (ICHRA evangelists), wait-and-see brokers, and traditionalists (resist change)
- **Broker-first platforms** (Vitable, Remodel Health) are specifically designed to protect broker relationships and commissions

## 4. The Cannibalization Concern

### The Core Tension
Carriers with large group health insurance books face a fundamental strategic conflict: investing in ICHRA infrastructure accelerates the migration of their own group customers to the individual market. The discount between commercial group rates and Health Insurance Exchange (HIX) rates drives the speed of cannibalization.

### Specific Carrier Concerns
1. **Revenue migration:** Small group commercial revenue shifts to lower-margin exchange products
2. **Risk pool fragmentation:** Younger, healthier employees may cherry-pick individual plans, leaving sicker populations in the group pool
3. **Competitive exposure:** Employers on ICHRA shop across carriers rather than being locked into a single group plan
4. **Broker channel disruption:** The traditional broker relationship (a key carrier distribution asset) is destabilized

### Who Wins, Who Loses
- **Winners:** Carriers already strong in the individual/ACA Marketplace (Centene, Oscar, Ambetter) -- ICHRA brings them employer-subsidized members
- **Losers:** Carriers without individual market presence who rely heavily on group business
- **Strategic imperative:** Oliver Wyman warns that if carriers don't present ICHRA as an option, someone else will. "Disrupt or be disrupted."

### The Math Driving Employer Switching
- PwC projects 8.5% medical cost increases for group plans in 2026
- Large renewal increases are the #1 trigger for employers switching to ICHRA
- Employers with high-risk employees can escape health-status rating in the group market by moving to ICHRA (individual market uses community rating under ACA)
- Defined contributions lock in employer costs vs. absorbing unpredictable carrier-driven rate hikes

### Strategic Narrative
HealthSherpa's pitch is essentially: as ICHRA scales, the administrative burden scales with it (unlike group insurance where one policy covers all employees). Manual, fragmented workflows break down. ## 7. The Build vs. Buy Question

### The Case for Building (with AI)
Recent AI advances (late 2025 - early 2026) have dramatically lowered the cost and complexity of building software:
- AI coding assistants can generate API integrations, enrollment workflows, and data pipelines at a fraction of historical cost
- LLMs can power plan recommendation engines (similar to Venteur's offering)
- AI can automate eligibility verification, compliance checks, and document processing
- 94% of payers are either live with or actively adopting AI, with 47% reporting widespread use

### The Case Against Building
1. **Regulatory complexity is the moat:** ICHRA involves ACA compliance, tax rules, CMS integrations, state-by-state marketplace variations -- this is not a simple API wrapper. AI can generate code but cannot navigate regulatory nuance without deep domain expertise.
2. **CMS trust is not replicable:** HealthSherpa's position handling 50%+ of Federal Marketplace enrollments represents years of earned trust and certified connectivity that cannot be replicated quickly.
3. **Network effects matter:** Connecting to 40+ ICHRA platforms through a single integration is valuable precisely because of aggregation. A carrier building its own connectivity must negotiate individually with each platform.
4. **Legacy system debt:** Carriers' legacy systems are data silos that "don't necessarily talk to each other." Building new ICHRA infrastructure on top of fragmented legacy systems compounds rather than solves the problem.
5. **Maintenance burden:** Home-grown systems become "exponentially expensive as technology advances" -- the total cost of ownership is far higher than the initial build.
6. **Speed to market:** ICHRA is at an inflection point now. Building takes 12-24+ months; buying provides immediate capability.
7. **Carrier core competency:** Carriers' expertise is in underwriting, risk management, and network development -- not enrollment technology infrastructure.

### The Nuanced Reality
- **Large carriers** (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Cigna) could theoretically build, but the opportunity cost and distraction from core business is high
- **Mid-size and regional carriers** almost certainly need to buy -- they lack the engineering resources
- **The AI acceleration risk for HealthSherpa:** If AI makes integration dramatically easier, the value of HealthSherpa's connectivity layer erodes over time. Their moat is the regulatory/CMS relationship, not the technology itself.
- **ICHRAx's standards approach** partially addresses this: if the industry adopts common standards, the need for a proprietary middleware layer diminishes

### Bottom Line
AI lowers the build cost but does not eliminate the regulatory complexity, CMS relationships, or network-effect advantages. For most carriers, buying remains the rational choice in the near term (2026-2027). The calculus may shift by 2028-2029 as AI capabilities mature and if ICHRA standards (via ICHRAx or similar) simplify the integration landscape.

## 8. Academic and Industry Research on ICHRA

### Peer-Reviewed / Quasi-Academic

**EBRI Issue Brief: "Individual Coverage HRAs: The Next Chapter in Employment-Based Health Benefits?"**
- Author: Paul Fronstin, Ph.D., Director of Health Benefits Research
- Key finding: ICHRAs are expanding access (especially among small businesses that never offered coverage) rather than displacing existing group plans
- Notes the 401(k) analogy: defined benefit to defined contribution transition
- Identifies a future recession as a potential catalyst for mass adoption

**Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker: "Explaining Individual Coverage Health Reimbursement Arrangements"**
- Authors: McGough, Rae, Cotter, Cox, Claxton
- Methodology: KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey + interviews with brokers, employers, insurers, ICHRA administrators
- Key finding: ICHRAs "have not taken off" -- still a very small share of employer health benefits
- Identifies network adequacy (lack of PPOs) and broker-vendor conflict as key barriers

**AJMC Interview with Matthew McGough (KFF)**
- Warns ICHRA growth could reshape marketplace risk pools and widen access disparities
- Notes employees struggle to navigate individual market shopping -- decision support tools are critical but lagging

### Industry Research & Strategy Papers

Most available research comes from industry think tanks (KFF, EBRI, Oliver Wyman), trade associations (HRA Council), and vendor-sponsored studies.

## 9. Key Pain Points (Carrier Perspective)

### "Our ICHRA strategy is half-baked"
- Many carriers acknowledge ICHRA is important but have not committed resources
- Internal teams lack ICHRA expertise; it falls between group benefits and individual market divisions
- No clear organizational ownership -- is ICHRA a group product, individual product, or something new?
- Technology infrastructure was built for group enrollment workflows, not individual market complexity
- Oliver Wyman warns: a half-committed approach may be worse than no strategy at all

### Cannibalization of group plans
- Small group commercial segment is the most vulnerable -- the discount between group rates and exchange rates accelerates migration
- Carriers with strong group books see ICHRA as a threat; carriers with strong individual market presence see it as an opportunity
- No clear framework for modeling the net financial impact (group revenue lost vs. individual revenue gained)
- Risk pool dynamics are uncertain -- will ICHRA bring healthier or sicker populations into the individual market?

### "We only care about the big platforms"
- 100+ ICHRA bentech players exist, but carriers have limited bandwidth for integrations
- Carriers want to partner with the 5-10 platforms that will ultimately win, not the long tail
- Difficulty identifying which platforms will survive -- the market is pre-consolidation
- This creates a chicken-and-egg problem: platforms need carrier integrations to grow, but carriers only want to integrate with platforms that have already grown
- HealthSherpa's value proposition directly addresses this by offering a single integration to 40+ platforms

### No clear ROI
- Carriers struggle to quantify the return on ICHRA investment
- Costs include: technology build/buy, compliance infrastructure, broker engagement, product development (individual plans with group-like features)
- Revenue is uncertain: individual market margins may be lower than group, and ICHRA members may switch carriers annually
- Attribution is difficult: how do carriers track which individual enrollments came through ICHRA vs. direct-to-consumer?
- Unlike group business (predictable multi-year relationships), ICHRA creates annual shopping dynamics
- The financial case often requires carriers to accept short-term losses for long-term market positioning

### Additional Pain Points
- **Broker channel disruption:** Traditional broker relationships (a key carrier distribution asset) are destabilized; carriers must develop new compensation models
- **State-by-state complexity:** Individual market options, network availability, and regulatory requirements vary dramatically by state
- **Consumer readiness:** Employees are not prepared to shop for individual coverage; carriers may need to invest in decision-support tools they've never built
- **Premium volatility:** 2026 premiums rising 17-30% on exchanges due to enhanced subsidy expiration -- makes ICHRA less attractive to employers precisely as adoption is accelerating
- **Regulatory uncertainty:** ICHRA exists by regulation, not statute -- though reversal is unlikely, it introduces uncertainty for long-term investment planning

## 10. Strategic Implications for the Consulting Engagement

### The Digital Experience Funnel Opportunity

Carriers need help across the entire ICHRA funnel:

**Awareness & Education**
- Most carrier teams don't fully understand ICHRA mechanics
- Internal education and strategic alignment (group vs. individual teams)
- Market sizing and opportunity modeling by geography

**Strategy & Product Design**
- Defining which employer segments to target (small group transition? large employer class carve-outs?)
- Designing individual market products that feel "group-like" (PPO networks, richer benefits)
- Modeling the cannibalization math: net revenue impact across group and individual books

**Technology & Infrastructure**
- Build vs. buy decision framework specific to each carrier's situation
- Integration strategy: HealthSherpa, ICHRAx, direct platform partnerships, or some combination
- API architecture for enrollment, quoting, status monitoring, and attribution

**Distribution & Broker Engagement**
- Broker segmentation (progressive vs. wait-and-see vs. traditionalist)
- Commission and compensation model design
- Broker enablement tools and training programs

**Enrollment Experience**
- Consumer-facing plan shopping and decision-support tools
- AI-powered plan recommendation engines
- Mobile-first enrollment experiences that match the expectations of individually-shopping consumers

**Analytics & ROI Measurement**
- ICHRA-specific KPIs and attribution models
- Group-to-individual migration tracking
- Competitive intelligence dashboards

### Key Messages for Carrier Executives
1. **The threat is real and quantifiable:** Centene estimates 45% of employer group market is at risk; Oliver Wyman projects 52% CAGR
2. **Inaction is the riskiest strategy:** "If carriers are not presenting ICHRA as an option to their group clients, someone else will"
3. **The infrastructure question is solvable:** HealthSherpa and ICHRAx offer viable buy options; AI reduces (but doesn't eliminate) the build cost
4. **This is a 3-5 year strategic investment,** not a quick-hit revenue play -- carriers must accept short-term investment for long-term positioning
5. **The 401(k) parallel is powerful:** Just as pensions gave way to defined contributions, group health will increasingly give way to defined-contribution models
