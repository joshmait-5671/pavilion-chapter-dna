A note from our founder

We are in it. Not approaching the edge of something — standing in the middle of it. The shift is real. The noise around it is designed. Most of what you're reading right now was written to make you act, not to help you think. I have spent ten years watching how the best operators in revenue navigate change: not by finding the right tool first, but by finding the right person. AI compresses time. It makes the work faster, the output better, the analysis cheaper. It does not compress wisdom. It does not make judgment cheaper or trust faster. Those still move at the speed of human beings — and always will. Pavilion exists for exactly that. The conversation that reframes the problem. The peer who tells you what didn't work. The relationship worth building before you needed it. Use the technology. Then find your people. That's what this has always been.

Sam Jacobs Founder & CEO, Pavilion

The Buddy Covenant

Every Buddy agrees
to show up right.

This is not a mentorship program or a lead generation exercise. Buddies opt in because they believe in the value of a good conversation. They agree to a simple set of standards.

  • Be open and honest About what you know, what you don't, and what has actually worked for you.
  • Be a good listener Understand the situation before you offer advice. Ask the second question.
  • Be direct If the match is not the right fit, say so. Don't leave someone waiting.
  • Be reasonable with your time One good conversation is the commitment. Where it goes from there is up to both of you.
  • Be vulnerable You don't have all the answers either. That is the whole point.
Why it matters

All information is now
potential misinformation.

X has a bias toward the provocative. YouTube rewards watch time. LinkedIn optimizes for engagement. The AI platforms themselves have an obvious interest in selling you on AI. Every source you consult is, in some way, working an angle.

That doesn't make all of it wrong. It makes none of it sufficient. You need a human sanity check. Someone who has no incentive to oversell you, who knows your context, and who will tell you when the thing doesn't work.

The people around you are going to matter more and more. Not less. The question is whether you are building those relationships now.

"The tools you use matter less than who you think alongside. Knowing what to use is downstream of knowing what you're trying to do. That's a people problem, not a software problem."
