We are in it. Not approaching the edge of something — standing in the middle of it. The shift is real. The noise around it is designed. Those still move at the speed of human beings — and always will. Pavilion exists for exactly that. The conversation that reframes the problem. The peer who tells you what didn't work. The relationship worth building before you needed it. Use the technology. Then find your people. That's what this has always been.

The Buddy Covenant

This isn't mentorship.
It's backup.

Every Buddy agrees to show up right. Not as a mentor. Not as a vendor. As a peer who's been in the room you're trying to get into.

  • Be open and honestAbout what's actually working and what isn't. Buddies aren't here to perform expertise. They're here to tell the truth.
  • Be a good listenerUnderstand the full situation before offering a take. Ask the second question. The obvious answer is rarely the right one.
  • Be directIf it's not a fit, say so early. Don't waste anyone's time. Directness is a form of respect.
  • Be reasonable with your timeOne real conversation is the ask. Where it goes after that is up to both of you. No pressure. No obligation.
  • Be vulnerableYou don't have all the answers either. Neither does your Buddy. That's exactly why the conversation is worth having.
Why it matters

Every source
has an angle.

X rewards the provocative. LinkedIn optimizes for engagement. The AI platforms themselves have an obvious interest in selling you on AI. Every source you consult is, in some way, working an angle.

That doesn't make all of it wrong. It makes none of it sufficient. You need a real sanity check — from someone with no incentive to oversell you, who understands your context, and who will tell you when the thing doesn't actually work.

The people around you are going to matter more as AI gets louder — not less. The question is whether you're building those relationships now or waiting until you need them.

"The tools you use matter less than who you think alongside. Knowing what to use is downstream of knowing what you're trying to do. That's a people problem, not a software problem."
